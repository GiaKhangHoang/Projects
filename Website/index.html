Grace Hopper

Date of birth: December 9, 1906

Date of death: January 1, 1992

Grace Hopper was a pioneering American mathematician and Rear Admiral in the U.S. Navy, renowned for her groundbreaking work in the development of computer technology. Hopper helped devise UNIVAC 1,the first commercial electronic com puter, and was instrumental in the development of COBOL (Common Business-Oriented Language), a programming language that became widely used in business applications.

After graduating from Vassar College with a B.A. in 1928, she attended Yale University,earning an M.A. in 1930 and a Ph.D. in 1934 in mathematics. Hopper taught at Vassar before joining the Naval Reserve in 1943, where she was assigned to the Bureau of Ordnance Computation Project at Harvard University in 1944. There, she worked on the Mark I, one of the earliest large-scale automatic calculators, and authored A Manual of Operation for the Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator (1946), the first comprehensive manual on how to program a computer.

In 1949, Hopper joined the Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation , where she helped develop one of the first compilers, a tool that translated programmer instructions into computer code. She coined the term "compiler" and contributed significantly to the UNIVAC I project. Later, while at Remington Rand (which later became Sperry Rand), she developed Flow-Matic, the first English-language data- processing compiler. This compiler laid the foundation for COBOL, which she championed and helped standardize.

Hopper retired from the Navy in 1966 but was recalled in 1967 to assist with standardizing military computer languages. She was promoted to commodore in 1983 and later to rear admiral in 1985. She remained the oldest active-duty officer in the U.S. Navy until her second retirement in 1986 at the age of 79.

Hopper was celebrated for her technical achievements and for mentoring younger generations in the field of computer science. In 1991, she was awared the National Medal of Technology, and in 2016, she was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom.
